Locating interstellar dust using Deep LearningShow Less

2021 Fall - 2022 Fall

Me and Michael Ofengenden worked with Zack Gainsforth from Space Science Labs on a project funded by NASA. We analyze the data collected by the NASA's Stardust mission and try to identity interstellar tracks using different kinds of machine learning algorithm.
We used Principle component analysis(PCA), Kmeans, and CNN to process the image and trained a model to identify tracks. We used sobel filter and double hough line transforms. Putting everything on a website using Streamlit, we are able to tune the parameter easily to find the best model.

Secondly, we helped Zack worked on his existed StoichiometryFitter, which is used to analyze the chemical phases for a given mineral composition. I use Flask to make a web app and using render to make it live. The original one is desktop app.

Dark Matter Radio in Berkeley Axions Work Show Less

2023 Summer - 2023 Fall

Link to Website

DM Radio is a Stanford-SLAC-Berkeley-MIT-North Carolina-Princeton collaboration to search for dark matter axions in the 5-100 MHz (20-400 neV). As with the microwave cavity experiments such as HAYSTAC, the experiment is based on the Primakoff interaction by which a pseudoscalar such as the axion can convert into a single real photon in the presence of a magnetic field. However in this case, the microwave cavity is replaced with a lumped-element LC resonator, which allows the experiment to probe to much lower frequencies. DM Radio 50 Liter will be operational in 2022; DM Radio Cubic Meter by 2025.

I worked on building the RLC circuit and then also write a Python script that can make the process of Dark Matter Radio(DM Radio) fully automatic. The DM radio is connected with a osciloscope and the circuits using a simple Arduino.
The Python scripts make sure the motor is functional.

Secular Chaos for a N-body Planetary System(Disk)Show Less

2024 Jan - present

Secular chaos has been newly identified as a key factor contributing to the eventual destabilization of celestial bodies such as Mercury within our solar system (Lithwick & Wu 2011), and the creation of hot Jupiters in extrasolar systems (Wu & Lithwick 2011). In celestial mechanics, "secular" refers to the orbit-averaged motion of a planet in secular theory, we study the motions of bodies not as individual point masses, but rather as interactions between massive, elliptical wires. Secular theory dates back to the 18th century dynamicists Laplace and Lagrange, but only in the modern era has it been realized that it can lead to chaos. This project aims to investigate secular chaos within an N-body planetary system. While 2-planet systems have been understood, the general case of N > 3 has not been explored. We seek to identify the global trends of secular chaos: whether a system becomes more or less chaotic as N increases while the individual planet mass decreases. Is a system of N = 10 Earths more or less chaotic than a system of 2 Jupiters? Understanding the global trends of secular chaos is vital for mapping out the dynamical behaviors of the endlessly diverse architectures of extrasolar systems across the universe.
